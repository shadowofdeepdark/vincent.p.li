About

I'm a final-year BSc Psychology student at the University of Edinburgh, specializing in psycholinguistics and experimental research on non-literal communication.

My research examines how people understand sarcasm and other forms of non-literal meaning in everyday communication. I'm particularly interested in how linguistic markers, contextual cues, and visual elements like emojis work together during real-time language comprehension.

Using eye-tracking methodology and experimental psycholinguistics, I investigate the cognitive mechanisms that allow readers to move beyond literal interpretation. My work combines behavioral measures with physiological data to understand how humans integrate information across modalities when interpreting meaning.

I work with Professor Martin Pickering, Professor Martin Corley, and Dr Patrick Sturt in the Department of Psychology at Edinburgh, where I'm preparing to pursue doctoral research in multimodal pragmatic interpretation.

Current Research

Parafoveal Effects of Emojis in Sarcasm Comprehension

Eye-tracking Multimodal

Supervised by Dr Patrick Sturt | Carnegie Trust Scholarship

Examining how emojis in parafoveal vision influence the processing of sarcastic statements during Chinese text reading. Using boundary-paradigm eye-tracking to measure real-time integration of visual and linguistic cues.

Status: Data collection complete (n=55), preliminary analyses show parafoveal emoji effects

Quotation Framing and Sarcasm Comprehension

Self-paced reading Pre-registered

Supervised by Professor Martin Corley

Investigating whether direct quotations (he said "this is great") facilitate sarcasm comprehension compared to indirect quotations (he said that it was great). Testing how narrative perspective influences pragmatic interpretation.

Status: Data collection ongoing, preliminary mixed-effects analyses underway

Communicative Context and Language Prediction

Eye-tracking Visual world

Supervised by Professor Martin Corley

Examining whether explicitly communicative contexts shift language predictions from typicality-driven to informativity-driven expectations, measured through visual attention patterns.

Status: Currently collecting data using EyeLink 1000 and OpenSesame

Prediction Under Adverse Conditions

Research Assistant ESRC-funded

Professor Martin Pickering's HollyMartin Lab

Multi-site collaboration investigating how cognitive load, noise, and hearing impairment affect prediction mechanisms in language comprehension. Coordinated Edinburgh site data collection.
