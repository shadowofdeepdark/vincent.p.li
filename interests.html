Interests

Research focus and methodological expertise

I'm a final-year BSc Psychology student at the University of Edinburgh, specializing in psycholinguistics and experimental research on non-literal communication.

My research examines how people understand sarcasm and other forms of non-literal meaning in everyday communication. I'm particularly interested in how linguistic markers, contextual cues, and visual elements like emojis work together during real-time language comprehension.

Using eye-tracking methodology and experimental psycholinguistics, I investigate the cognitive mechanisms that allow readers to move beyond literal interpretation. My work combines behavioral measures with physiological data to understand how humans integrate information across modalities when interpreting meaning.

I work with Professor Martin Pickering, Professor Martin Corley, and Dr Patrick Sturt in the Department of Psychology at Edinburgh, where I'm preparing to pursue doctoral research in multimodal pragmatic interpretation.

Research Interests

Sarcasm and Non-literal Language

Investigating how readers and listeners move beyond literal interpretation to understand intended meaning in sarcastic communication.

Multimodal Communication

Exploring how text, emojis, and visual cues interact during comprehension, particularly in digital communication contexts.

Eye-tracking & Real-time Processing

Using physiological measures to understand moment-by-moment language processing and integration of contextual information.

Linguistic Prediction

Examining how communicative context and expectations shape predictive processing during language comprehension.

Digital Pragmatics

Understanding how pragmatic interpretation adapts to digital communication platforms and online interaction.
